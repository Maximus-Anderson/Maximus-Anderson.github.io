CR29 Accumulator: Manufacture

Building Cyclone Racing's first tractive system from scratch: the aluminum housing, five battery modules, and everything that went wrong along the way.

Role

  • Manufacture
  • Validation

Specifications

  • Configuration: 28s1p per module
  • Cells: COSMX CA95B0D0HD
  • 5 modules total

Context

CR29 was Cyclone Racing's first electric car. The accumulator, the main high-voltage battery pack, had to be built from scratch. Nobody on the team had done this before. This write-up covers the full manufacturing process: the aluminum housing and the five battery modules that go inside it.

What an Accumulator Is

The FSAE rules called the main high-voltage battery pack the accumulator — that terminology shifted to "tractive battery" in the 2026 ruleset, but for CR29 it was the accumulator. It isn't one big battery. It's a housing containing smaller sub-units called modules, wired in series to reach the total pack voltage. Each module is its own self-contained group of cells. For CR29, each module holds 28 pouch cells wired in series with one cell per parallel group — 28s1p. Five modules in the housing make up the full pack.

The cells are COSMX CA95B0D0HD pouch cells — 13 Ah, 3.73 V nominal, 180 Wh/kg, 1.5 mΩ DCIR. Pouch cells are "flexible" in the sense that they're encased in a polymer film rather than a metal can like a cylindrical cell, but they're still quite rigid in practice. The advantage over cylindrical cells is meaningful: the large, flat electrode area gives a lower DC internal resistance, which means less resistive heating and better power delivery under load. The gravimetric energy density is also favorable, which matters for a car that has to stay under weight.

They do need careful handling, though. The polymer casing isn't as mechanically protective as a steel can. A dent, a crease, or a puncture can bring the anode and cathode into direct contact inside the cell, creating an internal short circuit. From there, the cell enters thermal runaway: a self-sustaining exothermic reaction that produces heat, gas, and eventually fire. Lithium battery fires are particularly bad because they generate their own oxygen and produce toxic gases including hydrogen fluoride — not that I'd know anything about that... The tabs — aluminum on the cathode side, nickel-plated copper on the anode — are not especially fragile as materials go, but bending or creasing them at the root can cause delamination where the tab meets the internal electrode, which is a different kind of failure.

The Aluminum Housing

The housing is a welded aluminum enclosure that holds all five modules, the BMS, and the high-voltage wiring. The flat panels were water jet cut from aluminum sheet, which gave clean, accurate profiles without introducing heat distortion.

The original design called for the side walls to be bent to shape rather than cut as separate pieces and welded. Bending would have been faster and produced cleaner corners, but when the parts came back from the shop, the bend tolerances weren't tight enough to be usable. The walls didn't sit where they needed to sit. Rather than redesign around the bending process, the bent sections were cut off and the walls were welded on as individual flat pieces instead, both the exterior panels and the interior partition walls.

That decision created a new problem. The exterior walls are 3.2mm thick and welded fine. The interior partition walls are a different story — 2.3mm, the rules minimum. Welding a full continuous seam into 2.3mm aluminum puts far more heat into the part than it can absorb without moving. The walls warped. After welding, the interior cavity was tight enough in places that the modules wouldn't fit. The fix was a mallet: carefully working the distorted spots outward until the modules could be coaxed in. The result was close enough to functional, but still out of spec dimensionally.

A lot of time also went into fitting and positioning each panel before tacking it in place so the assembled housing would match the CAD model. Even with that effort, the final housing still wasn't perfect. For CR30, the suggestion was to add slots to the mating edges so the panels self-locate when assembled, effectively self-jigging. That would cut fitment time significantly and remove the dependence on careful alignment before every tack weld.

Module Assembly

The 28 cells in each module get sorted into four types, A through D, based on tab length and orientation. The type determines where in the stack the cell goes. Getting the order wrong means a tab that doesn't reach its clamping plate, or one that's bent at the wrong angle.

Before touching a cell, the tab clamping plates had to be prepared. These are 3D-printed plates that clamp the cell tabs and allow them to be bolted together electrically. The inner-most bolt positions on each plate are inaccessible from below once cells are stacked on top, so the design called for press-fit nuts embedded in the plastic. In FDM-printed parts, press-fit doesn't hold reliably, so each nut got bonded in with JB Weld and left to cure. The intent was for them to behave like heat-set inserts, locking the nut in place so the bolt has something solid to thread into under clamping load.

With the plates ready, assembly went like this: drop the first D cell into the stack, lay a tab block over the tabs, fold the tabs up onto it, drop in the first A cell, fold its tab, add another tab block with a BMS sense wire ring, bolt through. Then repeat, alternating A and B cells through the stack. The final cell is a C type. After the last cell, main terminals get attached and the whole stack goes into polycarbonate baskets for structural support and isolation.

Total module build time ran about 5 hours a day over three days, which works out to roughly 3 hours per module including cell stacking, tab folding, bolting, and BMS sense wire harnessing. That doesn't count the 4 hours spent sanding burrs off the water jet-cut tab blocks before assembly could start, or the time spent crimping fuses onto the BMS wires separately.

What Didn't Work

The JB-welded nuts were the biggest module problem. Because they weren't sitting perfectly square in their pockets, some bolts cross-threaded on the way in. Once a bolt cross-threads into an epoxy-held nut, the nut breaks free. Now there's a loose nut somewhere inside a sealed module. Fixing it meant partially disassembling the stack mid-build. The bonded nuts also made disassembly difficult later when modules needed to come apart for CR30 work.

Stack pressure was the second gap. Pouch cells need to be compressed during cycling to prevent swelling and dendrite growth. The module design had compression plates for this, but the cell dimensions in CAD came from the datasheet rather than actual measurements. Real cells ran about 0.7mm thinner than spec per cell. Across 28 cells, that's roughly 20mm of lost compression. The plates that were supposed to clamp the stack weren't doing much.

The terminal bolts were a safety concern. The main pack terminal connections were accessible from below the module even with TPU plugs installed. Live conductors at those locations are a problem both for inspections and for any scenario where someone's hand ends up near the bottom of a live pack.

Notes for CR30

The full list flagged coming out of this build: add self-jigging slots to the housing panels so fitment doesn't require manual alignment, redesign the press-fit nut pockets with deeper and tighter interference fits so the nuts lock mechanically without JB Weld and have more wall contact so the plastic doesn't round out under bolt load, measure actual cells before finalizing compression plate dimensions, fully enclose the terminal bolt connections, reduce total bolt count in the module, and clean up the wiring harness routing inside the housing. The accumulator worked well enough to run subsequent testing. Building it showed exactly where the second-generation design needs to be different.

Dyno Cart Testing

Adapting Cyclone Racing's legacy IC dyno cart to validate an Emrax 208 motor and DTI HV550 inverter before the team committed to going electric.

Role

  • Design
  • Manufacture
  • Validation

Why This Existed

Cyclone Racing was finishing its last season as an IC team while beginning the transition to electric. The Emrax 208 motor was on hand, but we had no chassis to put it in and no way to run it under load. Before committing to abandoning a program we'd been dominant in, we needed to prove the electric powertrain actually worked. That meant spinning the motor with a full tractive system outside the car, which required somewhere to mount everything.

The team had a dyno cart that had been around so long nobody knew exactly when it was built. It had a Porsche sticker and a Ferrari sticker on the side, so we called it the Porsche Ferrari. It was configured for a Yamaha R6 engine, which meant it was useless for our purposes in its current state.

Reconfiguration

The original cart was built around the R6: IC mounting features, a gas tank, and basic IC wiring with no provision for GLV power or the HV interlocks needed to keep the tractive system live. The GLV battery cutting out would kill HV power to the tractive system, so that had to be managed carefully.

Rather than permanently converting it, I designed the reconfiguration to be swappable. IC mounting features were removed and replaced with bolted equivalents so the cart could be quickly switched between IC and EV configurations. The GLV wiring was pulled off the cart entirely and moved to a separate nearby cart, making the harness swap straightforward depending on what you were testing.

Motor Mount

The Emrax 208 is a pancake-style motor with a bolt pattern that didn't correspond to anything on the existing cart. I kept the mount simple: two lengths of square tube steel cut to span the full width of the cart to handle the motor torque, bolted at both ends, with a third piece cut at 90 degrees to capture three of the four motor bracket mounting holes and actually secure the motor. Nothing exotic, but it was rigid and did the job.

The Water Brake

A water brake is essentially a hydraulic absorption unit — water enclosed in a housing that acts like a pump, using fluid resistance to apply a counter-torque against whatever is spinning it. I chose it because the CR27 team had used one to complete a full-power test with the Yamaha R6, so I assumed it would hold up for the electric motor as well.

In hindsight I should have run hand calculations first. The data sheets for the water brake had been lost along with most institutional knowledge of the thing, and I'm not confident it was actually capable of the loads the Emrax can produce. It was sufficient for what we needed to confirm at the time, but future testing will use the 300 kW dynamometer the university has since installed, which gives actual load control and accurate torque curves. The Porsche Ferrari has probably earned its retirement.

Testing

The no-load test was the first time the full tractive system was spun up: battery pack, Orion BMS, electrical PCB code, and the DTI HV550 inverter all running together for the first time. The goal was to confirm everything communicated correctly, the BMS behaved as expected, and we understood how the system operated before putting any real demand on it.

The loaded test pushed higher power through the system with the water brake applying resistance. It surfaced an issue: the motor would stop randomly after about 30 seconds with no fault codes from the inverter or BMS. We worked through it, but it was a good reminder that a system that looks clean on paper has a way of surprising you the first time you actually run it.

The results weren't well documented, which is something I'd do differently. What we did confirm was that all the major components in the tractive system were functional and that the team understood how to operate and troubleshoot them — which was the whole point before committing to an EV program.

What's Next

Future powertrain testing will move to the university's proper dynamometer. Beyond the load limitations of the water brake, we've found that driver testing gives more useful data than anything we can simulate on the cart. The custom hub motors currently in development will spend time on the dyno before they go anywhere near a car, though that's a job for the 300 kW unit, not the Porsche Ferrari.

1976 Honda CB750F Restoration

Reviving a 50-year-old SOHC four: carburetors, electrics, timing, and the start of a cafe racer build.

Role

  • Diagnosis
  • Rebuild
  • Fabrication

Why This Bike

I came across a cafe racer build by Milezero Racer based on a CB750F Clubsport and immediately wanted one. Beyond the aesthetics, this was a project I genuinely needed. I'd learned about carbureted engines in theory but never had my hands on one, and carburetors are dated technology but the physics behind them are fascinating. There's also something satisfying about reviving a machine that hadn't turned over in years.

There's a practical reason too. I learn best by doing, and working through a real mechanical system, diagnosing problems, chasing faults, fixing things that fight back, builds intuition you can't get from a textbook. The CB750 has been one of the better teachers I've had.

Initial Assessment

The bike wasn't running when I got it but wasn't a disaster either. Tires were dry rotted, so I built a makeshift center stand from scrap wood and swapped them before anything else.

Then I ran through the basics: spark, compression, air, fuel.

Compression came in at 130–135 psi across all four cylinders. The SOHC forums put the healthy range at 150–180 psi, 120 being the low end of acceptable, and below 100 needing immediate work. The consistency across cylinders is a good sign, but it's on the low side and worth revisiting. The air filter was torn and got replaced. The carbs were the main concern: float pins seized from corrosion, so a simple clean wasn't going to work.

Electrical System

The electrics needed significant work. The headlight and tail light both had heavy parasitic draw, the high beams killed power to the whole bike, and the turn signals were dead. The previous owner had installed a lithium battery whose BMS couldn't handle the bike's demands, so I replaced it with an AGM while sorting everything else.

Tracing the harness with a multimeter turned up a collection of loose and corroded bullet connectors throughout. I cleaned or replaced the bad ones and ran continuity checks to confirm. Spark was weak but the coils tested fine; the coil boots had resistance far above spec at the plug interface, so those and the plugs got replaced.

For lighting I built a custom headlight from a Suzuki GS850 housing fitted with high-output LEDs and swapped the tail light. Before the swap, switching the lights on caused nearly half a volt of drop from the parasitic draw. After, the turn signals worked, draw was negligible, and the high beam issue was gone.

Carburetor Swap

The original carbs were too corroded to rebuild cleanly. I sourced a donor bike and stripped a set of Keihin 7A carbs, which are roughly equivalent in jetting to the originals. After cleaning, setting float levels, and syncing all four, I installed them.

They got the bike running but have been an ongoing problem. The carbs run rich, clog regularly, and lose their tune. I've retuned them six times and done a full rebuild with float level reset and jet cleaning four times. Plugs have fouled repeatedly as a result. Sorting the carbs properly is the top priority going forward.

Timing

With spark and carbs sorted the bike started, but only on three cylinders and would lug to a stop after a few minutes. I ran the static timing procedure from the SOHC forums: 12V test light on the points, engine rotated by hand with TDC/BDC confirmed by the markings and a finger in the plug hole. Timing was retarded on cylinders 2 and 3. After adjusting, all four fired cleanly. I skipped dynamic timing since the bike ran well after the static adjustment and I wanted to get miles on it. Something to verify properly down the road.

First Miles and What Still Needs Work

The first 100-mile ride went well enough to call it a success, but the remaining issues were hard to ignore. Fuel economy is poor from the rich-running carbs, and I have to run the petcock in reserve to get adequate flow. The clutch is notchy and needs a rebuild. The front brakes are unhappy, and the left caliper has a slow seep leaving a film on the rotor before each ride. It hasn't saturated the pad yet, which I haven't fully explained.

What's Next

Mechanical reliability first: clutch rebuild, brake overhaul, and properly sorting the carbs. After that, the cafe build starts. I want to fabricate a custom seat in carbon fiber nylon filament to cut weight and clean up the tail. The tank is getting a clear coat to preserve the steel and some of the patina. The mirrors vibrate badly at speed so I'm planning a lower-profile replacement with better isolation.

2 Week Tube Chassis

CR29's carbon chassis failed at demolding. Over spring break, five of us designed a replacement tube frame from scratch in two weeks. It never got built, but it was worth doing.

Role

  • Research
  • Design
  • Validation

How It Started

CR29 was designed around a carbon monocoque chassis. To speed up manufacturing, we built a welded steel mold instead of the usual fiberglass tooling. Steel is harder to seal, and during cure the vacuum bag lost pressure. Without full vacuum, the laminate didn't consolidate properly. When the chassis was demolded, it was immediately obvious — the surface looked crispy, layers visibly frayed at the edges. Press anywhere and it was soft, crunching because the fibers had no matrix holding them together. There was no repairing it. We knew right there at the mold that it was a total loss.

That left us with a competition season, a complete drivetrain and suspension package, and no chassis. Over spring break, myself and four others decided to try to salvage it.

The Sprint

No formal assignments upfront — we built a burndown list of everything the chassis needed, put it in front of everyone, and people grabbed tasks. The goal was to divide major subsystems so nobody was blocking anyone else.

The design wasn't starting from nothing. CR25 was the 25th car and one of the strongest competitive tube frames the program had built. Its geometry was the reference point. The objective for CR25-C was to match CR29's critical dimensions as closely as possible — driver position, overall envelope, and especially the mounting locations for the motor, wings, and suspension pickups. Those had all been carefully optimized for CR29, and changing them would mean revisiting work that was already done well. The tube frame was a workaround, not a redesign.

What I Worked On

I took cooling because the system was my original design for CR29. Moving to a tube frame changed the packaging constraints, not the system, so it made sense for me to adapt it rather than hand it off. The firewall went with it — the firewall rules in FSAE are specific about what has to be sealed and how, and getting it wrong is a technical inspection failure, so I took it to make sure compliance was handled carefully. I had been involved with both the firewall and seat on the original CR29 work as well.

Suspension was the bigger task. A tube frame is heavier than a carbon monocoque, which means the spring rates from CR29 wouldn't be appropriate anymore. I updated the spring selection to account for the added mass, re-ran the blade simulations the suspension team had developed to validate the geometry, laid out all the hardpoints, and carried over the A-arms, ARB, and struts from the original design. The goal was to touch as little as possible while making what needed to change actually correct.

At the end of the design phase, the team submitted an SES — Structural Equivalency Spreadsheet. It's an official FSAE-provided document that runs calculations to prove a chassis design meets the structural requirements of the rules. It's mandatory for competition; no SES approval, no tech sign-off. Submitting it was the formal checkpoint that the design was rules-legal.

The Vote

After the design was done and SES was in, the team had to decide whether to actually build it. That came down to funding and time. The vote went to the team leads and it wasn't clean — two tied votes, the meeting was essentially a war. Ultimately the decision came down in favor of bringing CR28, the last IC car, to competition instead. Optimizing a known, working car was a safer bet than manufacturing and debugging a brand new chassis in the time that remained.

CR28 went to Michigan and finished 5th. That's a hard result to argue with.

Looking Back

The two weeks were worth it. Not because the chassis got built, but because of what working that way felt like. A small group, a clear deadline, a list of tasks, no bureaucracy in the room — everyone just worked. It's the closest thing to a startup environment I've been in, and it confirmed that's the kind of team I want to be part of. The lesson that came with it is harder: you can do everything right and still lose the vote. A good solution to a bad situation doesn't guarantee it gets used. Sometimes the answer is correct and it still doesn't happen.

Using CR25 as the geometric reference and CR29's critical mounting locations as constraints, CR25-C was modeled over spring break. Subsystems were divided across the group: suspension, cooling, firewall, seat, and chassis structure, with each person working in parallel to avoid bottlenecks.

CR30 Battery Pack: Thermal Optimization for Endurance

How empirical DCIR testing on Cyclone Racing's CR30 cells replaced a complex liquid cooling system with ten fans — and why the spec sheet was nearly useless.

Role

  • Research
  • Design
  • Testing
  • Lead

The Problem with the Spec Sheet

The CR30 battery is a 5-module lithium-ion pack that needs to sustain current bursts up to 200A through FSAE endurance events powering the CR30 racecar. Heat rejection is critical — but when I went to calculate how much cooling the pack actually needed, I ran straight into a wall.

The cell spec sheet listed DCIR as either 1.5 mΩ or 5 mΩ — two discrete values with no curve, no conditions, no context. Depending on which you used, the thermal math either showed the pack severely overheating by the end of endurance, or barely needing any cooling at all. Assuming worst case to be safe, I started designing a liquid cooling system: U-shaped shims contacting cell faces, a dedicated cooling plate, thermal padding, quick disconnects, and a dedicated pump loop mounted to the pack.

The more I looked at it, the worse it felt. The design added significant weight and complexity, the cooling effectiveness would be marginal at best, and reliability on a racecar running a pack-dedicated fluid loop was a real concern. I was building an elaborate solution to a problem I hadn't actually confirmed existed. What I actually needed was to characterize the cells under real conditions, rather than trusting a spec sheet that clearly wasn't written with race applications in mind.

Building the Test Rig

I identified four variables that could meaningfully affect DCIR: stack pressure, temperature, C-rate, and state-of-charge. The goal was to isolate each one and understand how much it actually mattered, all for under $100.

  • Load Bank: Four 0.1 Ω heatsink resistors in a 2s2p configuration for uniform heating, validated at 0.131 Ω via 4-wire Kelvin sensing
  • C-Rate Tester: Carbon pile rheostat for variable current draw
  • Pressure Fixture: Machined steel plates with springs, adjustable from 0–8 psi
  • Current Sensing: Borrowed SSA-250 and current clamp from the PRISM Solar Car team

Not glamorous, but it covered the parameters I needed to test.

Testing Each Variable

Face Pressure — Ruled Out

Stack pressure was first. Using the spring fixture, I ran pulse tests at 0, 2, 5, and 8 psi across cell faces. DCIR barely moved. Given that the car sees a relatively low cycle count over its lifetime, pressure-driven SOH degradation isn't a meaningful design concern anyway — so I deprioritized it and moved on.

C-Rate — Secondary Effect

C-rate tests via the carbon pile rheostat showed DCIR trending linearly downward with increasing current draw, which I assume to be from local temperature increases due to ohmic heating. The effect was real but small relative to the other variables, so I didn't incorporate it into the core thermal simulations.

State-of-Charge — Repeatable and Strong

For SoC testing I ran 10-second discharge pulses to capture the ohmic voltage drop and 1RC transient, followed by 3-minute rests to track 2RC recovery. Theory predicts that as SoC drops, Li⁺ concentration in the electrolyte decreases and charge transfer kinetics at the electrode surface become less favorable — both of which increase resistance. The results matched: a clear, repeatable DCIR increase at lower states of charge.

Temperature — The One That Mattered

I didn't have access to the university temperature chamber when I needed it, so I improvised with a foldable temperature-controlled heat pad wrapped around isolated cells. Cell temperature was monitored with FLIR thermography and IR spot metering. I ran the same pulse protocol as the SoC tests across a range of roughly 25–60°C, and this is where things got interesting.

DCIR Mapping — The Spec Sheet Was Way Off

Temperature and SoC together dominated DCIR variance. When I plotted the full dataset, the results were genuinely surprising — DCIR dropped as low as 0.7 mΩ at operating temperature, which is less than half the spec sheet's stated minimum of 1.5 mΩ. The cells were running far more efficiently than the datasheet implied under real conditions.

I built a 3D surface in MATLAB mapping DCIR against temperature and SoC, then fit it to a third-order polynomial so it could feed into the thermal simulation the team was developing.

Running the thermal model with the real DCIR data — using a 1.5x factor of safety derived from RC recovery analysis — the pack's peak temperature stayed well below 60°C, the maximum allowable cell temperature under the rules, for the full endurance duration. The liquid cooling system wasn't just overkill, it was completely unnecessary.

Redesign

With the data confirming that passive heat mass was sufficient, I stripped out everything from the liquid system: the base plate, U-shims, thermal pads, pump, and radiator. In their place I added 10 brushless DC fans — 5 always-on for penthouse electronics cooling, and 5 BMS-relayed above the modules as a backup for cell cooling if needed. Regardless of whether the cell fans are active, airflow always intakes over the cell tabs and exhausts through the penthouse vents. Simple, lightweight, and the BMS relay gives it a real failsafe for anomalies like connection degradation.

Specific Heat — Freezer Calorimetry

There was no published specific heat data for these cells, so the team had been using aluminum as a stand-in for thermal simulations. I wanted to actually validate that before it fed into anything important. With some styrofoam, thermocouples, and my freezer, I set up a basic calorimetry test — first on aluminum blocks to establish a calibration multiplier, then on the cell itself. The results were surprisingly consistent across runs, landing at an average of 0.954 J/g°C. Not aluminum, but close enough that the simulations weren't wildly off.

Takeaways

The biggest lesson from this project is that spec sheets for cells like these are essentially useless for thermal design — they're written for general-purpose consumers, not race applications. Actually characterizing the cells under realistic conditions completely changed the design direction, saved meaningful weight, and gave the team a much better thermal model based on real data rather than conservative guesses. Sometime in the future I'd like to revisit this with proper temperature chamber access to fill in the edges of the DCIR map and run higher C-rate tests I couldn't reach with the resistor load bank.
