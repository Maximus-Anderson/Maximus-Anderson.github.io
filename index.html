FSAE Tractive Battery Module

This project involved the manufacturing of the team's first tractive system battery module and testing it to highlight key manufacturing inefficiencies and potential issues to improve in future packs.

Role

  • Manufacture
  • Validation

Specifications

  • Configuration: 28s1p
  • Cells: COSMX CA95B0D0HD

Background

For the ISU racecar, CR29, I was tasked with assembling key components of the accumulator, now referred to as the tractive battery, with a focus on the battery modules. These were designed to prioritize safety and repairability.

During manufacturing, I validated features such as press-fit nuts, identified issues like insufficient stack pressure, and noted potentially hazardous bolted connections below the main pack terminals, all of which were flagged for improvement in future designs. Despite these challenges, the module was successfully completed and has been utilized in dynamometer testing.

Project Timeline

March 20, 2025

Milestone 1: Inefficiencies highlighted

March 21, 2025

Milestone 2: Assembly

March 30, 2025

Milestone 3: Modules Completed

Due to the geometry of the tab clamping plates and bolts, the inner most nuts were meant to be press fit, and thus weren't accessible. To combat that JB weld had to be temporarily used to complete these modules. Further issues with cross threading were highlighted. Additionally, cells were on average 0.7mm thinner than the design leading to a lack of stack pressure.

Initial assembly was completed of the first module.

All modules fully completed with wiring harnesses and baskets.

Dyno Cart Testing

This project involved the design, manufacturing, and validation of a dynamometer cart to test the performance of the ISU racecar's tractive system and other components.

Role

  • Design
  • Manufacture
  • Validation

Background

The dyno cart was originally developed to facilitate testing of the ISU IC racecars, but had no functionality when the team migrated to EV. The project aimed to allow validation of the functionality of the tractive battery pack, Emrax 208 Motor, and DTI HV550 Inverter outside a complete car.

During the project, I designed a motor mount, reconfigured and manufactured the cart, and validated its functionality through testing.

1976 Honda CB750F Restoration

A full restoration of a 1976 Honda CB750F motorcycle, including carb rebuild, tune, compresion testing, timing, and electrical system upgrades. The project preserved the classic design while maintaining performance and reliability.

Role

  • Inspection
  • Rebuild
  • Validation

Background

I purchased this bike over the summer as a project bike. It wasn't running but was in beautiful condition with no visible rust or major corrosion. To get it running, I immediately checked for spark, compression, air, and fuel. Spark was weak and the battery wasn't holding charge, so I replaced the battery with an AGM, then spark boots and plugs, the air filter was torn so I installed a new one, compression was relatively low at 130-135 psi, but it was consistent, but the carbs were in rough condition with the float pins siezed due to corrosion.

I had a second set of carbs from a donor bike that I rebuilt that were in far better condition, so after syncing them and replacing jets to match the stock specs I installed those instead. Fairly soon after I managed to start the bike, but it was running off 3 cylinders. Spark was still weak so I traced the wiring harness and found loose connections. Spark was now good, but it would often misfire or lug to a stop after a few minutes. This led me to check timing, which was behind for the cyl 2-3. After changing static then dynamic timing, all 4 cylinders were firing and the bike seemed to be in great condition, and I was able to complete a 100 mile ride with essentially no issues.

New Keihin 7A carbs with new floats and pilot jets to match the original carbs were rebuilt and installed.

After re-assembly, the motorcycle was started and was able to complete a 100 mile ride with minimal issues.

2 Week Tube Chassis

After a carbon chassis failure, this rapid-design project to create a lightweight tube chassis matching the geometry of CR29 while maintaining rules compliance was completed in two weeks. The chassis was engineered for structural integrity and ease of manufacturing, we called it CR25-C.

Role

  • Research
  • Design
  • Validation

Background

CR29 was meant to have a carbon monocoque chassis. A new method of fabrication was employed to speed up the manufacturing process using a welded steel mold. This ended up having too much porosity causing the extreme delamination of layers. Due to this, over spring break a group of us attempted to salvage what was lost by creating a steel tube frame chassis that matched CR29's geometry as closely as possible.

The work was split up with each member taking over a subsystem. I was in charge of suspension as well as the firewall, seat, and the cooling system components I was previously responsible for. After making substantial progress and submitting SES, funding was evaluated and a vote was taken to decide whether we would pursure the manufacture of the chassis. It was decided that funding was too limited and the timeline too aggressive so the project was terminated.

Milestone 3: Project Termination
