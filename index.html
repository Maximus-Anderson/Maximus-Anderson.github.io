CR29 Battery Module: Manufacture

This project involved the manufacturing of the team's first tractive system battery module and testing it to highlight key manufacturing inefficiencies and potential issues to improve in future packs.

Role

  • Manufacture
  • Validation

Specifications

  • Configuration: 28s1p
  • Cells: COSMX CA95B0D0HD

Background

For the ISU racecar, CR29, I was tasked with assembling key components of the accumulator, now referred to as the tractive battery, with a focus on the battery modules. These were designed to prioritize safety and repairability.

During manufacturing, I validated features such as press-fit nuts, identified issues like insufficient stack pressure, and noted potentially hazardous bolted connections below the main pack terminals, all of which were flagged for improvement in future designs. Despite these challenges, the module was successfully completed and has been utilized in dynamometer testing.

To combat that JB weld had to be temporarily used to complete these modules. Further issues with cross threading were highlighted. Additionally, cells were on average 0.7mm thinner than the design leading to a lack of stack pressure.

1976 Honda CB750F Restoration

A full restoration of a 1976 Honda CB750F motorcycle, including carb rebuild, tune, compresion testing, timing, and electrical system upgrades. The project preserved the classic design while maintaining performance and reliability.

Role

  • Inspection
  • Rebuild
  • Validation

Background

I purchased this bike over the summer as a project bike. It wasn't running but was in beautiful condition with no visible rust or major corrosion. To get it running, I immediately checked for spark, compression, air, and fuel. Spark was weak and the battery wasn't holding charge, so I replaced the battery with an AGM, then spark boots and plugs, the air filter was torn so I installed a new one, compression was relatively low at 130-135 psi, but it was consistent, but the carbs were in rough condition with the float pins siezed due to corrosion.

I had a second set of carbs from a donor bike that I rebuilt that were in far better condition, so after syncing them and replacing jets to match the stock specs I installed those instead. Fairly soon after I managed to start the bike, but it was running off 3 cylinders. Spark was still weak so I traced the wiring harness and found loose connections. Spark was now good, but it would often misfire or lug to a stop after a few minutes. This led me to check timing, which was behind for the cyl 2-3. After changing static then dynamic timing, all 4 cylinders were firing and the bike seemed to be in great condition, and I was able to complete a 100 mile ride with essentially no issues.

2 Week Tube Chassis

After a carbon chassis failure, this rapid-design project to create a lightweight tube chassis matching the geometry of CR29 while maintaining rules compliance was completed in two weeks. The chassis was engineered for structural integrity and ease of manufacturing, we called it CR25-C.

Role

  • Research
  • Design
  • Validation

Background

CR29 was meant to have a carbon monocoque chassis. A new method of fabrication was employed to speed up the manufacturing process using a welded steel mold. This ended up having too much porosity causing the extreme delamination of layers. Due to this, over spring break a group of us attempted to salvage what was lost by creating a steel tube frame chassis that matched CR29's geometry as closely as possible.

The work was split up with each member taking over a subsystem. I was in charge of suspension as well as the firewall, seat, and the cooling system components I was previously responsible for. After making substantial progress and submitting SES, funding was evaluated and a vote was taken to decide whether we would pursure the manufacture of the chassis. It was decided that funding was too limited and the timeline too aggressive so the project was terminated.

CR30 Battery Pack: Thermal Optimization for Endurance

In a previous iteration of Cyclone Racing's EV racing project, the CR29 battery module exposed critical gaps: undocumented design choices, manufacturing defects, and unverified thermal assumptions. Moving to the CR30 pack demanded more than iteration—it required deconstruction to fundamentals. Why a passive heat mass system without active cooling? What drove cell resistance in real conditions? With a $100 budget and three-week deadline, I led a redesign grounded in physics: heat as I²R losses, dissipation via conduction and convection, and decisions validated by data, not speculation.

This project transformed CR29's lessons into CR30's architecture, blending passive thermal mass with targeted active cooling. No off-the-shelf datasheets, only vague notes stating general ranges in normal environments, inspiring testing and simulation to ensure reliability under race loads.

Role

  • Research
  • Design
  • Testing
  • Lead

Specifications

  • Budget: $100
  • Timeline: 3 weeks
  • Target: <60°C peaks
  • Load: Up to 200A pulses

What is the CR30 Battery Pack?

The CR30 is a modular lithium-ion pack for our university EV racecar, comprising 5 segmented modules. It powers sustained high current bursts (up to 200A) over endurance events, where poor thermal management can be grounds for disqualification. Drawing from CR28 telemetry, the design prioritizes low weight, reliability, and fault tolerant cooling.

My initial design leveraged spec sheet data and the CR29 design. Calculations showed severe overheating within the pack by the end of endurance. Getting sufficient airflow would be difficult due to rules constraints, so I developed a worst case design mirroring commercial pack dedicated liquid systems and micro extruded coolant channels. Given space constraints, a new approach was taken and a less conventional design was chosen incorporating U shaped shims contacting cell faces and a separate cooling plate, with thermal padding to increase surface contact. This design featured quick disconnects and a pack dedicated cooling loop mounted to the battery.

This design frustrated me—its effects would be poor at best and it added substantial weight, complexity, and reduced reliability. I'm designing a battery for a racecar so it shouldn't mirror a commercial pack with dedicated liquid systems. CR30 needed evolution through empirical DCIR (DC Internal Resistance) characterization, to reveal that overbuilt liquid cooling was unnecessary for race loads.

Project Goals

My objectives balanced performance, constraints, and scalability:

  • Validate and optimize thermal management for <60°C peaks under worst-case pulses and confirm the cell spec sheet listed 1.5-5 mΩ DCIR range
  • Integrate CR29 manufacturing insights to eliminate defects
  • Resource efficiency: <$100 test rig
  • Failsafe integration: BMS-triggered cooling for anomalies like connection degradation

Pulse Test Rig: Building Under Constraints

I prioritized parameters: stack pressure, temperature, C-rate, and state-of-charge (SoC). The $100 rig included:

  • Load Bank: Four 0.1 ohm heatsink resistors (2s2p) for uniform heating, validated to be 0.131 Ω via 4-wire Kelvin sensing. Ideal for test pulses, excluding high C-rates.
  • C-Rate Tester: Inexpensive carbon pile rheostat
  • Pressure Fixture: Machined steel plates with springs (0-8 psi)
  • Current Sensing: Borrowed SSA-250 and current clamp from PRISM Solar Car

Parameter Tests: Isolating DCIR Influences

With the rig operational, I systematically isolated effects on DCIR, starting with mechanical factors and progressing to electrical and thermal drivers.

Face Pressure: Minimal Impact Ruled Out

Stack pressure was first: Using the steel plate-spring fixture, we applied 0-8 psi to cell faces, simulating compression variances. Pulses revealed negligible DCIR shifts. Given the anticipated low cycle counts for the vehicle's life, SOH degradation wasn't a design criterion. Pressure was deprioritized, freeing resources for higher-impact variables.

Current Draw: Modest Effect, Not Dominant

Next, C-rate tests via the carbon pile rheostat. DCIR trended linearly downward with increasing draw, attributable to ohmic heating. However, the effect was secondary and not incorporated into core simulations, as thermal/SoC drivers overshadowed it.

State-of-Charge: As Predicted

10-second pulses for ohmic drop and 1RC transient, plus 3-minute rests for 2RC recovery and monitoring. Effects were generally as expected with repeatable and strong effects.

Temperature: Pivotal Characterization

University temperature chamber access was delayed, so I adapted a foldable temperature controlled heat pad over isolated cells. Using FLIR thermography and IR spot metering the temperature of the cell was noted. The procedure matched the SoC tests from ~25-60°C.

Data-Driven Modeling: From Pulses to Polynomials

Temperature and SoC dominated DCIR variance. The plotted data was completely unexpected. Using MATLAB I created a 3D surface of DCIR vs. temp/SoC, then fit it to a third-order polynomial to be used in a new thermal sim the team was developing.

My RC analysis through recovery phases suggested 1.3x FoS; conservatism yielded 1.5x. Despite that, the calculated maximum temperature was below 60 degrees celsius, so I concluded heat mass suffices for race durations, rendering liquid cooling obsolete.

Redesign Synthesis: Hybrid Cooling Architecture

In the redesign I removed all pack specific liquid cooling items: the base plate, U-shims, pads, pump, and radiator. I then added 10 brushless DC fans (5 BMS-relayed for penthouse electronics per spec sheet data, 5 BMS-relayed for tab airflow directly above modules). I placed the intake through cells, and exhaust via penthouse vents, a simple failsafe.

Specific Heat Discovery

There was no data available on the specific heat of the cell so the team was using values matching aluminum. I decided I wanted to validate that and with some extra styrofoam, thermocouples, and my freezer… I managed to get some surprisingly consistent data for quick calculations. I conducted tests on aluminum blocks, then a cell. After creating a multiplier based on the aluminum measured values, an average of 0.954 J/g°C was found.

Conclusion

I learned a lot through this project. From how batteries work to the 2-RC model. I really enjoyed the process and hope to refine these data and procedures when I have some free time.
